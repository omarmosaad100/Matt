BEDOUIN MARKET

By Heba Magdy

Imagine walking around the streets of Maadi late at night, looking for something to eat, perhaps some entertainment, but bored nonetheless. You take random lefts and rights, leading you to nowhere in particular. The streets are serene at night, many small shops and restaurants are still buzzing with last calls, and other people, foreign and local are taking similar random strolls in the city that truly does not sleep.

One of those random lefts or rights brings you to a wide street with buildings on both sides, small shops nestled to the buildings ground floor, with narrow, colorful entrances. You happen upon a little service road with a parking full of dormant cars, and hidden from view, is a small shop, with a dozen steps leading to its front door. Colorful woven carpets and pottery lay haphazardly on the floor, with a makeshift wooden couch that looks too stunning to be left outside, but all to uncomfortable to be used as an actual seat for any person, but apparently, it proves to be more than convenient for a tray dog who is fast asleep on it. You go inside and are greeted by an assortment of accessories, bags, fabrics, secondhand books, trinkets, gadgets, artwork, furniture, and the list truly goes on. All or most of Bedouin origin. The Bedouin market is a very old shop that has been open in Maadi since 1985, and since the 50â€™s in the Areesh area. It has collected dust due to negligence and fewer customers, but the owner and sole employee is very warm and welcoming to anyone with an interest in antiques. The prices are low, the pool of choice is wide and deep. So, you must ask yourself, why is it that such a place has fallen through the cracks? Why do we not care about things made with the blood and sweat of Egyptians, with such an elevated skill and knowledgeable craft?

It is truly a curiosity, but the existence of a much more saturated industry that produces fake counterparts to everything provided in the Bedouin market is a reason why we as people maintain our reckless shopping spree fantasies in pursuit of all things with no depth, history, or true art. The only people you see inside the place, are travelers, taking with them small parts of the shop to give to people who do not see these things on a daily basis, who will appreciate them undoubtedly. We as a people are the reason for the underrepresentation of the diverse culture that produces this work. And the Bedouin market is physical proof that this truly needs fixing.
