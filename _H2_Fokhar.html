Fokhar.. The Art of patience

By Halla Hossam

They call it the art of patience, because in a hectic and rushed world focused on immediacy and speed, a potter’s work relies on a rare virtue: patience. Some say it’s an art of simple pleasure, if is formed when clay and patience coalesce. No two pots are ever alike when it comes to pottery. The fractures in the clay, the shape that morphs as hands cup together, and the pieces of ourselves that we transmit into its creation all tell stories. Pottery is an ancient Egyptian art that encompasses a wide range of disciplines. Ancient Egyptians were masters of various arts, but pottery was their specialty. An item from ancient Egypt is this clay pot. It even appeared during Egypt's predynastic period, which is the earliest period in recorded history. Each age had diverse procedures of manufacture, new materials, distinct styles, forms, and colors, demonstrating the transforming character of the art of pottery in Egyptian history. Each epoch, each location, may have its own individual style. Fakharani is the name given to a man who works in the pottery industry (after the word fokhkhar, meaning pottery). A seasoned fakharani knows which shapes are popular in which seasons. He must be able to create the shape of jars

used during birth festivities, as well as the incense burner over which the mother must step in subu, the celebration celebrated on the seventh day after a baby's birth. He can make jars in the shape of horses and roosters. He can also make fruit basins with human faces on them. The secrets of the craft are frequently kept within the Fakharani family. To be a fakharani, you must come from a family of potters. Most of the pottery factories in Egypt are located mainly in Old Cairo, around the area of Darb 1718, one of Egypt’s cultural hubs.

Pottery does not have the place in Egyptian society it once had, it doesn’t have a place in the spotlight as other art forms as well as a job, but its artisans continue to innovate, they make their art and sell it to others.

We find that pottery as an art form is very underrated and underrepresented in Egypt and we find it to be our duty to shed the light on such an artistic and creative job.
