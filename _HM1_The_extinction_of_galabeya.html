The Extinction of Galabeya

By Heba Magdy

Any cultured individual who cares about history, Egyptian history in particular, concurs that it is a wildly diverse country. And one of the aspects that has garnered a lot of attention is the country’s fashion, whether past or present, it is interesting to say the least. And while today’s fashion is born mostly out of aesthetic and statements, the fashion of the past was born of simplicity and practicality. And one of the most famous, but least talked about articles of clothing that has an intense evolution is the traditional Galabeya of Egypt’s Nile and farming communities.

Slightly different in cut from the robes worn by other Arab cultures, the galabeya is a loose-fitting, wide-sleeved garment worn by both women and men alike. It is incredibly light and breezy, making it comfortable and adequate for the whether we all know Egypt is famous for. Under the Ottoman and European rule, the traditional garment thrived and held on, the desert and coastal tribes and the Nile basin communities stuck to their altered forms of the galabeya with impunity. And while it remains seen to this day, iconic as ever, for both its diversity and simplicity, we were not counting on the onslaught of a very persuading enemy; western fast fashion.

And while western fashion is not a direct influencing factor in the decline of the galabeya in and of itself, it quite clearly spread in central Cairo and dominated, covered the existence of the traditional garments. The streets of Cairo are a wonder to the foreign eyes, in a single blink, you will see an older man wearing the tradition navy blue, deep olive, or brown galabeya, a woman wearing a much more colorful version of a long sleeved galabeya with a basket filled with goods balanced on top of her head, a group of young boys wearing ripped skinny jeans and oversized sweaters, and a woman wearing joggers and a tank top. It may be confusing, but the underlying fact is that this has been the case for years, one way or another, we have coexisted with differences usually undisturbed by the contrast. So, yes, it is quite confusing, but it garners a lot of respect, the diversity and continuous evolution and development is admirable, because even when the new is in, the old is never out.
