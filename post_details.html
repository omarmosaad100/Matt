Souq El Gomaa "We sell every old thing"

All my life I would hear about Souq El Gomaa and stories about it, but never have my feet stepped on its land until April of last year, when I decided to go there and explore that place that I knew nothing about but others' tales.

All my life I would hear about Souq El Gomaa and stories about it, but never have my feet stepped on its land until April of last year, when I decided to go there and explore that place that I knew nothing about but others' tales.

The place that has all what may come to one's mind, used cloths, used shoes, used houseware, for a very cheap price that suits the pockets of the members of the marginalized class.

When I arrived to the Souq's borders, I had an overview but it gave me a little. Many crowds overlapping each other, at least to my sight, but when I stepped in, my sight widened to discover each block.

The place is divided to squares, each one is specified doe selling something. One for selling used clothes, one for damaged electronics, and one for selling used phones, many of which may be stolen, you never know. All of that definitely for a very low price that fits attracted customers.

In the morning of each Friday, just after the sunrise, all the sellers gather from everywhere and display their goods on the floor waiting for passing shoppers.

In the place specified to selling used clothes you find all types of clothes for both genders, socks, under-wears, and even wedding dresses. Prices are variable but all in a low range. There is a popular sentence you would hear in Egypt "Koll haga liha zebonha" .. Everything has got its customer.

And there is a place for houseware where you find soon-to-be-married women checking items before buying since you wont be able to return it post-buying. Most likely because you will never see the seller again.

In the Souq, there is everything the sellers and buyers would need to enjoy their day. For example, it's very popular there Egyptian national food, you may find cars selling Egyptian oven pasta, Mahshy if you know what that is, and ice-cream. In the winter you would find cars selling Humus.

Just once you step your first leg in Souq El Gomaa, you get to know it's a different world, has its own regulations and special people, those who live in the margin, they don't plan for tomorrow because they have no time to do so, they don't have dreams or goals, their only goal is to survive the day.
